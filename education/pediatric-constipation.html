Pediatric Constipation

A practical guide for families: what constipation is, what to watch for, and what helps.

What is constipation?

  • Hard, painful stools or infrequent stools.
  • Stool withholding (child avoids the toilet) is common and can worsen constipation.
  • Soiling (encopresis) can happen when soft stool leaks around a hard stool blockage.

Red flags (contact a clinician)

  • Blood in stool (more than small streaks) or black stool.
  • Poor growth, weight loss, persistent vomiting, severe abdominal distension.
  • Constipation starting in the first month of life or delayed passage of meconium.
  • Fever, severe pain, or child appears very unwell.

Home strategies

  • Toilet routine: sit 5–10 minutes after meals (especially after breakfast and dinner).
  • Feet supported (stool/footrest) so knees are higher than hips.
  • Fluids and fiber: aim for regular water; add fruits/vegetables/whole grains gradually.
  • Reward the routine, not the result (sticker chart works well).

Medicines (laxatives)

  • Many children need a “softener” for several months to break the cycle of withholding.
  • Common options include polyethylene glycol (PEG) or lactulose (your clinician will guide dosing).
  • Do not stop suddenly once improved; taper slowly when stools are soft and regular.
Important: If your child has severe pain, vomiting, a distended abdomen, or can’t pass stool/gas, seek urgent care.

When to seek urgent help

  • Severe abdominal pain, vomiting, dehydration, or a very swollen abdomen.
  • Blood in stool with weakness, dizziness, or large volume bleeding.
  • Concern for bowel obstruction or child looks very unwell.
